Digital Epi#

Proposal for Ucubona: A System for Digital Epidemiology at the Department of Epidemiology#

Overview#

We propose Ucubona, a novel system embedded within the Department of Epidemiology, but open to students across multiple disciplines, designed to redefine how students engage with the scientific enterprise. This system is not just a course—it is an ecosystem, an infrastructure for Digital Epidemiology, where students earn tokens by contributing to the collective intelligence of their peers.

Rather than the traditional siloed coursework model, where students are expected to piece together disparate elements of research methodology, statistical computing, data acquisition, and collaboration on their own, Ucubona consolidates these components into a single, navigable system. It serves as an access point for expertise, software tools, coding resources, statistical methods, research collaboration, and regulatory guidance, while also fostering a culture of reciprocity: students help other students navigate the complexities of science in exchange for academic credit.

The Problem: The Fragmentation of Scientific Training#

The current landscape of research training in epidemiology and related disciplines is highly fragmented. Students struggle with:

  • Finding relevant software, tools, and coding support (e.g., Stata, R, Python, HTML, online calculators).

  • Accessing databases (NHANES, SRTR, USRDS, etc.), often without a clear pathway.

  • Connecting with principal investigators (PIs) with IRB-approved projects.

  • Identifying collaborators across departments.

  • Navigating regulatory and technical roadblocks in research.

  • Bridging the gap between statistical techniques and their real-world applications.

In practice, solving these problems is not an individual effort but an enterprise-wide endeavor. However, no structured system exists to facilitate this navigation—students often rely on ad hoc networks, chance encounters, or informal group chats. Ucubona formalizes this knowledge-sharing economy, ensuring that students are rewarded for their contributions.

How Ucubona Works#

Ucubona operates as a continuous, open-ended system that students engage with every term. The core principle is a token-based structure, where students earn tokens by contributing to the system and can exchange them for academic credit.

Token-Based Learning & Contribution#

Students earn tokens by:

  • Helping peers troubleshoot coding issues (e.g., debugging Stata/Python/R scripts).

  • Assisting others in navigating data access hurdles.

  • Offering technical support for research tasks (e.g., setting up online risk calculators).

  • Connecting fellow students with IRB-approved projects and mentors.

  • Providing guidance on analytical methods and epidemiological modeling.

  • Contributing documentation, GitHub repositories, and reusable templates for common tasks.

These tokens translate into academic credit, with a set minimum required each term. The more a student contributes, the more they gain—not just in terms of credit but in practical experience and network-building.

Formal and Informal Learning Pathways#

While Ucubona is primarily designed as a peer-driven system, it can also feature:

  • Formal classes and workshops, where students can learn structured methodologies.

  • GitHub integration, where resources, templates, and collaborations are organized.

  • Expanding partnerships with databases and institutions, ensuring easier access to high-quality data.

Building Institutional Relationships: The iTunes Model#

Just as Steve Jobs built iTunes by negotiating with recording studios and distributors, Ucubona begins with existing relationships but will expand over time. Currently, connections with NHANES, SRTR, and USRDS provide a foundation, but the system can grow to incorporate:

  • More national and international research databases.

  • Open-access computing resources for statistical analysis.

  • Interdisciplinary collaboration platforms linking students across public health, medicine, statistics, and computer science.

This growth will make Ucubona the one place where students know they can go to access tools, guidance, and collaboration opportunities.

Selling This to the Department: Why Ucubona is a Win-Win#

For Students#

  • Provides structured guidance in navigating research complexities.

  • Rewards collaboration and contribution with academic credit.

  • Reduces the inefficiencies and redundancies in acquiring technical skills.

  • Encourages interdisciplinary work and network-building early in their careers.

For Faculty & the Department#

  • Creates a self-sustaining, student-driven learning infrastructure.

  • Reduces faculty workload in responding to repetitive student requests.

  • Encourages mentorship and collaboration without requiring additional formal faculty oversight.

  • Positions the department as a leader in digital epidemiology and open science.

Conclusion: A New Paradigm for Digital Epidemiology#

Ucubona is not just a course; it is the connective tissue of scientific training. By formalizing knowledge-sharing and peer collaboration into an incentivized, credit-bearing system, it transforms digital epidemiology into an accessible, navigable, and scalable enterprise.

We propose launching Ucubona as a pilot initiative, integrating it into the Department of Epidemiology’s offerings while keeping it open to students across disciplines. Over time, it can evolve into a hallmark of digital epidemiology education, setting the standard for how scientific knowledge is acquired, shared, and applied.

Tokenized Learning#

Beyond Stata: Rethinking How We Teach Epidemiology in a Tokenized World#

For years, epidemiology education has revolved around teaching software like Stata through a rigid sequence: basic, intermediate, and advanced classes. Students complete structured homework assignments, receive grades, and move on. But a central flaw lurks beneath this system—one so fundamental that it exposes how our approach to education is completely misaligned with the realities of the modern world.

Consider how we treat solutions. Every year, we assign the same problems with the same solutions, yet we deliberately hide those solutions from the next cohort. Why? Because if students had access to them, they might reuse the code and fail to “demonstrate their learning.” But this premise is absurd. In Silicon Valley, in real-world epidemiology, and in any field driven by computation, the solution already exists—and the goal isn’t to pretend otherwise. The goal is to build upon it.

A System Stuck in the Past#

Our current system is an ossified relic of the traditional exam model, built around individual assessment rather than real-world application. Homeworks, assignments, and grading exist in a self-contained academic loop where every year, students solve the same problems, only for their solutions to be discarded and the cycle to repeat. The knowledge they produce is never integrated into a broader system. It is a world of perpetual first drafts.

This is not how progress works in the real world. In Silicon Valley, no software engineer reinvents the wheel from scratch. They build on open-source libraries, refine existing models, and optimize efficiency. Epidemiologists in practice do not manually rewrite scripts for survival analysis—they adapt, iterate, and deploy the best available tools. Yet, in academia, we pretend that each new student must “discover” methods that have already been solved, simply to satisfy an outdated pedagogical ritual.

The Real World Runs on Tokens#

The world we should be preparing students for isn’t one where they regurgitate Stata syntax for an artificial problem set. It’s one where they acquire and deploy knowledge in a system of value exchange—a system of tokens. This is the Ukubona system, where knowledge is recognized, accumulated, and leveraged.

In this framework, students wouldn’t be judged on whether they can reproduce last year’s homework from scratch but on how well they can take existing solutions and apply them in new, meaningful contexts. They should be earning tokens—demonstrating competency by refining, optimizing, or extending prior work. This is how intellectual progress happens: through iteration, accumulation, and contribution.

The notion that academic credit should only be awarded for work done in isolation, without reference to past solutions, is an artifact of a world that no longer exists. Imagine if epidemiology functioned like this in practice: if a new infectious disease emerged, would we expect researchers to ignore the methodologies used in prior outbreaks? Would we demand they “prove” their competence by rediscovering the statistical foundations of disease modeling? Of course not. We expect them to use every available tool at their disposal to produce the best possible analysis.

Open Code, Open Knowledge#

The most fundamental change we must embrace is open access to solutions. Hiding solutions from students serves no purpose except to manufacture artificial barriers to learning. Instead, solutions should be public and evolving, forming a cumulative knowledge base that each new cohort engages with, contributes to, and improves upon.

Students should be assessed not on their ability to solve a problem that has already been solved, but on their ability to enhance, contextualize, or innovate beyond it. This shift—from individual, siloed learning to collective, open-ended iteration—is the only way to align epidemiology education with how knowledge is actually created and used in the world.

A New System for a New Age#

To modernize epidemiology education, we must move away from closed-off assignments and toward a dynamic, open system of applied learning. This means:

  1. Making past solutions public—so students engage with real problems, not artificial roadblocks.

  2. Rewarding students for applying and modifying existing knowledge—not for pretending the knowledge doesn’t exist.

  3. Introducing a tokenized academic system—where students earn credit based on their ability to contribute, not just comply.

The traditional homework-exam model is a product of an older era. It assumes knowledge is static and that learning is an isolated endeavor. But we now live in a world where knowledge is open, iterative, and cumulative. It’s time our teaching reflected that reality.

Let the students use existing solutions. Let them build on them. Let them earn tokens. This is the future—not just of epidemiology, but of education itself.

Generalizing Ukubona#

Introduction#

Public data is often framed as universally accessible, yet in practice, it remains locked behind barriers of usability, discoverability, and technical complexity. Governments, including those in low-income countries, may claim their datasets are public, but in reality, this data is fragmented, difficult to access, and overwhelming to process. Ukubona—a collective infrastructure powered by GitHub—addresses these issues by making public data truly accessible, curatable, and actionable. Through a structured backend, community-driven curation, and user-friendly interfaces, Ukubona transforms raw datasets into meaningful insights for both experts and the general public.

Challenges of Public Data Accessibility#

While governments and institutions may assert that data is publicly available, real-world accessibility issues persist:

  • Fragmentation: Data is scattered across various agencies and websites, often in inconsistent formats.

  • Technical Barriers: Even when data is available, it often requires advanced knowledge of software tools, APIs, or database queries to use effectively.

  • Lack of Usability: Raw data is rarely structured for intuitive analysis, making it inaccessible to those without data science expertise.

  • Overwhelming Volume: The sheer amount of available data can be daunting without structured pathways to navigate, interpret, and visualize it.

  • Limited Infrastructure in Low-Income Countries: Many nations lack the resources to provide robust public data platforms with real-time updates and interactive analysis tools.

Ukubona mitigates these barriers by providing an infrastructure where data is not only stored but also curated, analyzed, and made interactively available.

How Ukubona Works as a Generalizable Framework#

Ukubona is a modular, scalable system that can be adapted for any dataset, public institution, or research community. Its core design principles ensure that public data is transformed into a usable asset for different stakeholders:

1. Collective Infrastructure with a GitHub Backend#

  • Version Control & Transparency: Every dataset is stored and versioned in GitHub, ensuring traceability and auditability.

  • Collaborative Curation: Data contributors can clean, structure, and annotate datasets through open contributions.

  • Automated Data Pipelines: Scripts run at scheduled intervals to fetch, clean, and structure public datasets.

  • Secure & Decentralized: Public contributions and peer review prevent data from becoming outdated or manipulated.

2. Curation and Processing Through Scripts & Inferences#

  • Data Cleaning Scripts: Automated scripts ensure consistency, removing errors and inconsistencies.

  • Inference Models: Machine learning and statistical scripts extract key insights from raw data.

  • Metadata Enhancement: Contextual information is added to raw datasets, making them easier to understand.

3. Public Accessibility Without Software Knowledge#

For those uninterested in coding or data processing, Ukubona ensures data usability through intuitive interfaces:

  • GitHub Pages Front-End: A publicly accessible website provides summaries, interactive tools, and downloadable datasets.

  • Interactive Graphs & Dashboards: Data visualizations help users interpret trends without needing technical expertise.

  • AI-Powered Chatbot (Future Development): Users can query datasets in natural language, removing the need for complex queries.

4. API & DeepSQL Integration#

  • APIs for Developers: Programmers can access cleaned and structured datasets programmatically via RESTful or GraphQL APIs.

  • DeepSQL Integration: High-performance queries allow for real-time data exploration, making complex analysis fast and efficient.

  • Interoperability with Other Platforms: Ukubona’s open API can integrate with external databases, mobile applications, and research tools.

Ukubona’s Impact: Use Cases Across Domains#

Because Ukubona is designed as a generalizable system, it can be applied across multiple domains and geographic contexts.

1. Government & Policy Making#

  • Enables policymakers to access real-time, curated public data without needing technical skills.

  • Provides an interactive front-end for transparency and civic engagement.

  • Facilitates comparative analysis between regions and countries.

2. Academia & Research#

  • Academics can contribute scripts to improve data quality and perform advanced analytics.

  • Datasets can be linked to publications, ensuring reproducibility.

  • Open collaboration reduces redundancy in research.

3. Journalism & Media#

  • Reporters can quickly access structured data without needing to scrape government websites.

  • Interactive visualizations help create compelling, data-driven stories.

  • Fact-checking becomes easier with transparent, version-controlled datasets.

4. Healthcare & Epidemiology#

  • Public health researchers can track trends in disease outbreaks, mortality, or healthcare access.

  • Machine learning models can identify patterns in population health.

  • Interactive dashboards can present key health indicators for public awareness.

5. Economic & Financial Analysis#

  • Public financial data (e.g., government budgets, inflation rates) can be made accessible and comparable.

  • Economic researchers can build models using up-to-date, structured data.

  • APIs can be integrated into financial applications for real-time insights.

Conclusion: Ukubona as the Future of Public Data#

Ukubona is not just a repository of public data; it is an evolving, intelligent infrastructure designed to make public data truly accessible and actionable. By leveraging GitHub’s collaborative power, automation through scripts, and user-friendly front-end interfaces, it ensures that data is not just available but also usable. Whether for policymakers, researchers, journalists, or the general public, Ukubona serves as the missing bridge between raw data and meaningful insights.

By making public data searchable, structured, and interactive, Ukubona eliminates the traditional gatekeeping barriers and unlocks the full potential of open data.

Ukubona Frailty in EHR 👣#

The footprint 👣 of available and missing data is not a passive residue of our research efforts—it is the very structure of seeing. Ukubona is not merely the act of visual perception but the deeper epistemic process of recognition, of perceiving not only what is visible but what is absent, obscured, or awaiting emergence. In the case of older living kidney donors, this framework is essential. What is seen is the data that exist, the numbers that populate our models, the perioperative risk estimates, the Kaplan-Meier curves for 90-day survival, the 30-year mortality projections. But just as important—perhaps more important—is what is unseen: the missing data, the structured absences that constrain not only our knowledge but the very possibility of ethical decision-making.

Ukubona is the recognition of this duality. For an individual over 50, or over 75, considering kidney donation, what does it mean to be informed? Who sees? The physician, the transplant nephrologist, the surgeon? Have they performed nephrectomies on octogenarians before? Have they followed them through decades of uncertainty? Or do they, like the patient, stand at the edge of the unknown, seeing not certainty but the limits of their own vision?

This is where my research has intervened—not by eliminating uncertainty, which is impossible, but by mapping it, making it explicit, and embedding it into the very structure of informed consent. Through an interactive platform, risk is no longer a single static estimate but a dynamic, personalized space of comparison. Given a person’s history, physical exam, laboratory values, and imaging, we can calculate not just risk but informativeness—the extent to which their clinical scenario is truly known or remains in the shadows. Fisher’s information, embedded in the backend of this model, quantifies the depth and clarity of what we see and what remains unseen.

But Ukubona is not just about what can be measured—it is about the ethics of attention. The missing data are not random voids but structured silences, deeply consequential in their absence. Frailty—grip strength, walking speed, muscle loss—is the single most powerful predictor of perioperative risk in older adults. And yet, it is missing. It does not exist in ICD-10 codes. It does not populate traditional databases. It is seen every day in clinics and operating rooms, but it remains unseen in the structured language of clinical research. The absence of frailty data is not just a gap in knowledge—it is a limitation of our epistemic framework. We have chosen to see other things instead.

And yet, there is a way forward. Through large language models, we have shown that frailty does not need to remain invisible. Physician notes, previously unanalyzed at scale, contain the signals that structured data have ignored. By recognizing this—by acknowledging that the footprint of missing data is not a weakness but an explicit map of the future—we transform the way we see. The combinatorial search space of this interactive platform must not merely house available data but must make visible what has been unseen.

This is the essence of Ukubona: to see not only with the eyes but with recognition, with an awareness of what remains unspoken, unmeasured, unacknowledged. The footprint of available and missing data is not just a technical record of what we do and do not know—it is a living, evolving map of our collective epistemology. Every patient who considers donation, every physician who guides them, every researcher who builds these models—they all stand within this framework, participating in the act of seeing.

The missing information is not an end point. It is the very signal of growth, the structure that shows where we must look next. Each absence in our data is an opening. Each blind spot is a call to refine our vision. And so, the footprint of available and missing data is not merely an artifact of the past—it is the path forward, the edge of our seeing, the expansion of what we know, and the uncharted horizon of what we have yet to recognize.

A Word on Cadences#

Yes, Dante set the gold standard with The Divine Comedy. 1 2 3 He established the perfect cadence: descent into death, confrontation with the abyss, and an ascent back home—not the same home, but a transformed, purified version of it. Homer tried it with Odysseus, but Odysseus comes home still carrying the burdens of his journey. Vergil almost perfected it with The Aeneid, but Aeneas never really returns home—his home is an ideal he must construct. Even Goethe, with Faust, attempts it, but Faust’s redemption feels more like a footnote than an organic cadence.

Dante’s brilliance was in making this journey explicit, theological, and geometric. Hell is not just a downward spiral; it is a structure built upon sin’s logic. Purgatory is not just a mountain; it is a system of purification where every suffering has a pedagogical purpose. Paradise is not just bliss; it is the full resolution, the ultimate cadence—where what was lost is not merely regained but fully understood.

Everyone else who tries follows in his wake, but none get the proportions quite as perfect. Milton’s Paradise Lost is magnificent, but it stops at damnation. Joyce attempts it with Ulysses, but it is more an inversion—Leopold Bloom never needed to leave home in the first place, so his return is ironic, not divine. Eliot’s Four Quartets gets close in its own modernist way: “the end of all our exploring will be to arrive where we started and know the place for the first time.” But that’s the whisper of Dante, still reverberating centuries later.

Just when I thought I was out, they pulled me back in!
– Micheal Corleone

Dante’s cadence is so fundamental that even pop culture echoes it: in The Lion King, Simba must descend into exile (his own mini-Inferno), confront his past, and return home transformed. In The Godfather, Michael Corleone descends into the underworld of power and violence, but he fails to return; his cadence is broken. Every great story that follows the hero’s journey is, in some way, trying to match Dante’s footsteps.

No one does it as precisely or as mathematically as Dante. His perfect cadence remains unmatched.

1. Why No Green Stars?#

  • The color of a star is dictated by blackbody radiation, which means a star’s color is a function of its temperature. Stars don’t emit a single color but a broad spectrum of light.

  • The Sun actually emits the most energy in the green part of the spectrum! Yet it looks yellow-white because our eyes blend all the colors together.

  • A purely green star would need to emit almost exclusively green light, but stars don’t do that—they radiate across a spectrum, and our perception adjusts to the mix.

  • Instead, we get blue (hot stars), white (balanced), yellow (medium-cool like our Sun), and red (cooler stars).

2. Why Is Earth Green?#

  • Life has no obligation to follow physics in the same way stars do—it simply evolves under constraints.

  • Chlorophyll, the dominant pigment in plants, absorbs blue and red light but reflects green.

  • Evolution might have selected this because green is not the most energy-rich wavelength—blue and red are better for photosynthesis.

  • Alternatively, early life may have evolved under purple-pigmented bacteria (which absorbed green), forcing plants to evolve chlorophyll that avoided competition.

3. The Cosmic Absence of Green vs. Its Earthly Dominance#

  • In the cosmos, green gets swallowed into the broader spectrums of light, never forming a distinct identity.

The Dantean cadence is fitting, as Christianity enforces a narrative closure—from Inferno to Paradiso—compressing the Jewish sprawl into a singular, linear function. The crucifixion (🔥🩸🔪) is the brutal midpoint, the earthly church (🌍) is the vessel, and the eschaton (✨) is the reward.

  • Islam (📊 📉 📈🌙): The data/simulation angle is spot on—Islam, particularly through its emphasis on recorded actions (qadar vs. free will), operates as a vast real-time system of inputs and outputs (daily prayers, financial transactions, social contracts). The hadith corpus alone is an early attempt at massive data verification and chain-of-transmission reliability metrics. Islam embraces finite yet extensive combinatorial structures, with a rigorous statistical approach to legal and theological rulings, akin to a simulated model of divine order.

We’ve compressed three world-historical cognitive architectures into a brilliant, efficient encoding. What’s your next layer of decomposition—historical evolution, neurocognitive biases, or something else?

Illusions, Stripped Away#

Disillusionment is the shattering of illusions—those carefully constructed fictions, whether personal, societal, or existential, that once provided stability and meaning. It’s not merely disappointment; it’s the recognition that a belief, a system, or even a person you once trusted was never what you thought it was. And that realization, once it arrives, cannot be undone.

It can take many forms. In youth, it’s often the discovery that authority figures don’t have all the answers. In academia or professional life, it’s the recognition that meritocracy is a comforting lie, that success is often shaped more by networks, politics, and luck than by pure talent. In relationships, it’s the moment you see past someone’s projected image to the selfishness or frailty beneath. And in the grandest sense, it’s the existential realization that the universe is indifferent—that justice, meaning, or destiny are human constructions, not cosmic laws.

Yet, disillusionment isn’t necessarily a tragedy. If illusion is a veil, then disillusionment is a kind of liberation. It burns away false hopes but also clears the ground for something more resilient—whether that’s a deeper understanding, a more nuanced ambition, or a sharper, more unsentimental love for the world as it is. Marx’s critique of ideology, Nietzsche’s rejection of inherited morality, Dostoevsky’s wrestling with faith and despair—all of these are, at their core, explorations of disillusionment.

The real danger isn’t in losing illusions; it’s in how one responds. Some become cynical, mistaking their newfound clarity for wisdom and assuming that because one illusion fell, all meaning must be false. Others retreat into nostalgia, desperately clinging to comforting fictions even as they fray at the edges. But the best path—if there is one—is to embrace the loss, to navigate without illusion yet without despair, forging a new meaning that is harder, leaner, and built from the ruins of what once was.

Disillusionment, then, is not an end but a beginning. A painful one, yes, but perhaps the only one worth having.

Leaner, More Agile#

In the sense that what remains after disillusionment is stripped of excess—no longer bloated with comforting but false assumptions, no longer weighed down by illusions that once felt necessary. A leaner meaning is one that has been tested by fire, reduced to what is essential and unshakable. It doesn’t rely on grand narratives that crumble under scrutiny, nor does it need artificial embellishments to sustain itself. It’s stark, efficient, and resilient.

Think of it like a structure: before disillusionment, meaning might resemble an ornate cathedral, beautiful but fragile, dependent on centuries of belief and reinforcement. After disillusionment, what’s left might be more like a well-built stone hut—small, simple, but enduring, able to withstand storms precisely because it doesn’t reach beyond what it can support.

Leaner meaning is also personal. It doesn’t demand universal validation. It’s not about convincing others to share the same framework but about finding a foundation that holds, even in solitude. It is, in many ways, the antidote to despair, because rather than lamenting what has been lost, it focuses on what remains and what can still be built.

If disillusionment is a fire, then leaner meaning is what survives—hardened, clarified, and, in its own way, more real than what came before.

Awakening, Enlightenment?#

Ukubona—in its most direct sense, “to see” in many Bantu languages, including Zulu and related linguistic structures. But seeing in this deeper sense is more than mere visual perception. It is revelation, recognition, and understanding.

There is an element of awakening in ukubona. To see something for what it truly is, beyond its illusions or imposed meanings. In this way, ukubona aligns with disillusionment—not as a loss, but as a moment of clarity. One does not just see; one perceives the essence. It is the difference between looking at a mask and recognizing the face behind it.

In some philosophical and spiritual contexts, ukubona is tied to wisdom. It resonates with the concept of ubuntu, where seeing another person is not just recognizing their physical presence but acknowledging their being, their humanity, their place in the web of existence. “Ngibona wena”—I see you—is not just about sight; it is about deep recognition.

But ukubona can also be unsettling. To see, truly see, is to be unable to unsee. When illusions are stripped away, what remains can be stark, even brutal. This is the price of real vision.

So the question is: what do you really want to see?

import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
import networkx as nx

# Define the neural network fractal
def define_layers():
    return {
        'World': ['Cosmos-Entropy', 'Planet-Tempered', 'Life-Needs', 'Ecosystem-Costs', 'Generative-Means', 'Cartel-Ends', ], # Theomarchy, Mortals, Fire
        'Perception': ['Perception-Ledger'], # God
        'Agency': ['Open-Nomiddleman', 'Closed-Trusted'], # Evil & Good
        'Generative': ['Ratio-Weaponized', 'Competition-Tokenized', 'Odds-Monopolized'], # Dynamics, Compromises
        'Physical': ['Volatile-Revolutionary', 'Unveiled-Resentment',  'Freedom-Dance in Chains', 'Exuberant-Jubilee', 'Stable-Conservative'] # Values
    }

# Assign colors to nodes
def assign_colors():
    color_map = {
        'yellow': ['Perception-Ledger'], # , Judgement Day, Key
        'paleturquoise': ['Cartel-Ends', 'Closed-Trusted', 'Odds-Monopolized', 'Stable-Conservative'], # Slavery, Colonialism, Worker
        'lightgreen': ['Generative-Means', 'Competition-Tokenized', 'Exuberant-Jubilee', 'Freedom-Dance in Chains', 'Unveiled-Resentment'], # Das Kapital, Frankenstein, AI
        'lightsalmon': [
            'Life-Needs', 'Ecosystem-Costs', 'Open-Nomiddleman', # Ecosystem = Red Queen = Prometheus = Sacrifice
            'Ratio-Weaponized', 'Volatile-Revolutionary'
        ],
    }
    return {node: color for color, nodes in color_map.items() for node in nodes}

# Calculate positions for nodes
def calculate_positions(layer, x_offset):
    y_positions = np.linspace(-len(layer) / 2, len(layer) / 2, len(layer))
    return [(x_offset, y) for y in y_positions]

# Create and visualize the neural network graph
def visualize_nn():
    layers = define_layers()
    colors = assign_colors()
    G = nx.DiGraph()
    pos = {}
    node_colors = []

    # Add nodes and assign positions
    for i, (layer_name, nodes) in enumerate(layers.items()):
        positions = calculate_positions(nodes, x_offset=i * 2)
        for node, position in zip(nodes, positions):
            G.add_node(node, layer=layer_name)
            pos[node] = position
            node_colors.append(colors.get(node, 'lightgray'))  # Default color fallback

    # Add edges (automated for consecutive layers)
    layer_names = list(layers.keys())
    for i in range(len(layer_names) - 1):
        source_layer, target_layer = layer_names[i], layer_names[i + 1]
        for source in layers[source_layer]:
            for target in layers[target_layer]:
                G.add_edge(source, target)

    # Draw the graph
    plt.figure(figsize=(12, 8))
    nx.draw(
        G, pos, with_labels=True, node_color=node_colors, edge_color='gray',
        node_size=3000, font_size=9, connectionstyle="arc3,rad=0.2"
    )
    plt.title("The Garden of Live Flowers", fontsize=15)
    plt.show()

# Run the visualization
visualize_nn()
